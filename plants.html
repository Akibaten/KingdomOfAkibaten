Persephone (she/her)

Coleus scutellarioides

I got Persephone from a local nursery. She is absolutely gorgeous. Coleus plants have incredibly interesting variegation. The variegation (its pattern) and the color of a coleus is decided by light levels. Red coloration is from production of anthocyanins, and this occurs when the coleus is stressed from too much light. Anthocyanins work as a natural UV protector for the leaves that stops them from getting sunburned. So if a coleus is in the shade its leaves will be primarily green, and if its in direct sunlight, its leaves will slowly shift towards primarily red.

Athena (She/her)

Epipremnum (Pothos) aureum

Athena is my marble queen pothos which is another one golden pothos cultivars I mentioned in my description for Jade. Athena is new to me, but she is so so so pretty. I felt like Athen was the perfect name for her just given how bold she looks. She is finally one I can hang in my room, and I feel like she really brings the place together :) This gives me a chance to give more facts about Pothos. Pothos very rarely flower, sexually reproduce, and fruit. There's a myth that the last time flowering was seen in a plant from the genus Epipremnum was in 1964, but I can't find anything that actually supports this claim. Modern day houseplant pothos just have a mutation that prevents them from flowering. Different cultivars/types of pothos come from random mutations in the same pothos instead of sexual reproduction which is how we normally selectively breed (like selecting the biggest corn to reproduce so we get huge corn plants or seedless bananas). Because pothos lack the gene to flower and sexually reproduce, it is fair to say that a large large large majority of all the pothos you see in people's houses or even in the wild are the SAME plant! Isn't that crazy? they are all just the same plant having a vine cut off, propagating and growing a new plant. This means that outside of random genetic mutations almost all pothos are genetically identical and from the same couple of plants that could be hundreds of years old.

Juno (They/Them)

Mostera deliciosa

Juno is my monstera! They are actually getting pretty big now! They recently unfurled a leaf that was just beautiful :o Monsteras are fascinating plants. I could talk about them forever. Monstera deliciosa, despite being pretty big compared to other climbing plants they are still climbers and will climb up trees, trellises, etc. The genus Monstera actual has several species that are pretty small and vine-y. The most common is probably Monstera adansonii. The key trait that makes Monstera identifiable is the holes in their leaves called fenestrations. We don't know exactly why these fenestrations exist. It might be to make leaves less likely to get ripped apart by winds or maybe to let water fall to the roots of the plant, but they are still quite unique looking and can be quite pretty. here is a leaf my monstera recently unfurled that I thought was quite pretty. Monstera deliciosa are quite special because they can fruit! They fruit so easily you can do it at home, and supposedly the fruit is super tasty here is a picture of a guy picking and eating its fruit!

Ceres (She/Her)

Aglaonema modestum

Ceres is my aglaonema! Aglaonema's are angiosperms and like all angiosperms (pothos, monstera, tulips, corn, so so many etc) it produces fruit which scientifically is just a fertilized ovary that becomes a thing that has seeds and disperses them somehow. Aglaonema fruits are poisonous, but they are still quite pretty. Aglaonemas also can have extremely pretty foliage ranging from pink to red to silver to this super cool tricolor camoflauge pattern.

Intro

Being trans is hard and confusing and weird. I'm extremely passionate about the monolith that is the trans experience. I wanted to make this as a hub of resources relating to trans healthcare, a dangerously frequently misunderstood area of medicine, as well as my experience transitioning such as what led me to transitioning, what the experience itself was like, what was hard, what was really rewarding, and my understanding of psychological what was happening to me in terms of identity and self understanding and where I am now. If you are questioning your gender and maybe even wonder if you are trans, please know there is nothing wrong with you, you have nothing to be ashamed of, and even if realize you aren't trans that is okay and just as valid as being trans. What does it mean to be transgender?

What it means to be transgender does not have a universally accepted definition between researchers. Its a really complicated topic and is tied to tons of culture and that complicates things a lot.
Being transgenderI'm not going to use a term like transgenderism because its derogatory/has an extremely negative conotation and isn't used in literature or anything is a general term that does have a commonly agreed upon general definition: the state of one's gender identity not aligning the sex that was assigned to them at birth. So for example, someone can be recorded as being male when they are born but go on to reject that and instead decide or come to the realization that they are actually a woman. A trans person that was recorded as being male or female when born is typically referred to as AMAB (Assigned Male At Birth) or AFAB (Assigned Female At Birth) respectively; however, for the rest of this guide I'm going to use the designation Presumed instead of Assigned as I think its more accurate (so PMAB instead of AMAB or PFAB instead of AFAB), but it is essentially the same thing.

There are tons of ideas about the nuance of a person being transgender or who is trans and who isn't or whats required to have that label etc etc. I will get into all of these later but they aren't important right now. A person who is trans typically transitions to the gender or gender expression they feel best suits them. Transition (this process of transitioning) is super multifaceted and includes two main types of changes: social and medical. Social transition is changes a person makes to what clothes they wear or their name or what pronouns they want people to use to refer to them, and medical transition is changes a person fundamentally makes to their body like taking hormones or surgery. Transition is not one size fits all. Some people might not change their name. Some might not get surgery. Some might choose to not transition medically and only transition socially. None of these are inherently wrong. People just want different things and thats okay.
